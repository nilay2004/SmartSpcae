# Introduction

The proliferation of web-based applications and the increasing demand for intuitive spatial design tools have revolutionized how individuals and professionals approach interior design and architectural planning. Traditional computer-aided design (CAD) software, while powerful, often presents significant barriers to entry including steep learning curves, high computational requirements, and substantial licensing costs. Moreover, the disconnect between two-dimensional floor plan representations and three-dimensional spatial understanding has long been a challenge in both professional and consumer-facing design applications. As the global interior design market continues to expand, with projections indicating substantial growth driven by urbanization and the increasing emphasis on personalized living spaces, there exists a critical need for accessible, web-based solutions that bridge the gap between conceptual design and realistic visualization.

Contemporary web technologies, particularly WebGL and modern JavaScript frameworks, have enabled the development of sophisticated 3D graphics applications that run entirely within web browsers, eliminating the need for specialized software installations. However, creating a comprehensive interior design application that seamlessly integrates 2D floor plan editing with real-time 3D visualization presents numerous technical challenges. These include maintaining geometric consistency between 2D and 3D representations, implementing efficient spatial data structures for room and wall management, ensuring real-time rendering performance, and providing intuitive user interaction paradigms that accommodate both novice and expert users.

Existing solutions in the domain of web-based interior design can be broadly categorized into two classes: simplified tools that offer limited functionality and fail to provide accurate spatial representations, and complex professional-grade applications that require extensive training. The former often lack the geometric precision necessary for practical design work, while the latter remain inaccessible to the broader consumer market. Furthermore, many current implementations suffer from architectural limitations that prevent extensibility, making it difficult to integrate additional features such as automated space optimization, material cost estimation, or integration with building information modeling (BIM) systems.

This paper presents Blueprint3D, a novel web-based 3D interior design framework that addresses these limitations through a carefully architected system combining interactive 2D floor plan editing with real-time 3D visualization. Our approach leverages a half-edge data structure for robust geometric representation, enabling efficient room detection, wall management, and spatial relationship computation. The system implements a modular architecture that cleanly separates concerns between data modeling, 2D floor plan manipulation, and 3D scene rendering, facilitating both ease of use and extensibility.

The primary contributions of this work are fourfold. First, we introduce a unified geometric model that maintains consistency between 2D floor plan representations and 3D scene geometry, ensuring that modifications in either view are accurately reflected in the other. Second, we present an efficient item placement system that supports multiple item types—including floor items, wall-mounted items, and in-wall fixtures—with collision detection and spatial constraint enforcement. Third, we demonstrate a real-time rendering pipeline optimized for web browsers that achieves smooth interaction while handling complex scenes with multiple rooms, numerous furniture items, and dynamic lighting. Finally, we provide a modular, extensible framework architecture that enables developers to easily integrate additional features and customize the application for specific use cases.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews related work in web-based 3D graphics, interior design applications, and geometric modeling techniques. Section III presents the system architecture and design principles underlying Blueprint3D. Section IV details the core algorithms and data structures, including the half-edge representation, room detection algorithms, and item placement mechanisms. Section V describes the implementation details, including the integration of Three.js for 3D rendering and the interactive floor plan editor. Section VI presents experimental results demonstrating the system's performance, usability, and capabilities. Section VII discusses potential extensions and future work. Finally, Section VIII concludes the paper.
